Kaylee Bliss

  "For this creative project, I painted a picture of an infiltrometer. This painting is significant to me because it represents the large amount of time I devoted to testing infiltration and everything I learned during the process. In our class, I spent the most time out of everyone with this device and I really enjoyed getting to learn how it works. Getting it to work correctly was quite a challenge, as some of the soil we worked with was super dry and hard to sink the penetrometer into, while other soil was very wet and caused leaking during tests. Setting up the device through all of the mulch was very difficult, and even caused one infiltrometer to break. We then learned that instead of hammering on the handle of the device, a plank of wood should be placed over top first to avoid damage.
  In addition to the challenges we faced, the use of the infiltrometer itself is extremely time-consuming. For each plot, the test must run four times for 5 minutes each time. This made for an incredibly lengthy testing process, as there were 16 regular plots and 20 control plots that required testing. In addition, many of the days we worked were close to freezing temperatures, which made the time feel even longer.
  After my work with the infiltrometer, I gained a much deeper appreciation for the science of ecology. While the process itself wasn’t super exciting, seeing the results were. It was really neat to see for myself what infiltration was like on the site, and to make inferences on why it might be this way. For example, sites near erosion channels tended to have very low infiltration rates, which could be why the channels formed instead of foot traffic like we originally thought. The trial and error of setting up the infiltrometer was also an important learning experience and forced me to come up with solutions to the problems. I felt like my work on this project was super valuable, and I believe that this is a unique experience I would have never even known existed if not for this class."
